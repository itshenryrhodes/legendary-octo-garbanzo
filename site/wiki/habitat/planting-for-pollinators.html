Planting for pollinators

Design a bee-friendly garden or landscape with continuous nectar and pollen from early spring to late autumn.

Core principles

  • Continuity: ensure blooms across the whole season—early, mid, and late.
  • Diversity: mix flower shapes (open, tubular, composite) and colours.
  • Native-first: prefer locally adapted species; add hardy herbs and shrubs.

Even a small patch helps if planted densely and pesticide-free.

Garden structure

  • Trees & shrubs for early forage (willow, fruit trees, hawthorn, mahonia).
  • Perennials for summer backbone (echinacea, rudbeckia, sedum/stonecrop).
  • Herbs for sustained bloom and aroma (thyme, rosemary, sage, lavender, mint).

Water & shelter

  • Provide shallow water with landing stones; refresh weekly.
  • Leave some rough ground, leaf litter, and hollow stems for nesting and overwintering insects.
  • Windbreaks (hedges/fences) create warm flight corridors.

Bloom calendar (examples, UK-leaning)

Early season (Feb–Apr)

  • Willow (Salix spp.), hazel, flowering currant, mahonia, hellebore, crocus, snowdrop.
  • Herbs under cloches: chives, thyme (early flushes).

Mid season (May–Jul)

  • Fruit trees, hawthorn, bramble, clover, borage, thyme, sage, foxglove, catmint.
  • Ornamentals: single-flowered roses, cosmos (single), cornflower.

Late season (Aug–Oct)

  • Heather, ivy, sedum (stonecrop), asters, rudbeckia, goldenrod, verbena bonariensis.
  • Veg/herbs: mint allowed to flower, courgette blossoms.

Choose single-flower forms; double/pompom flowers often hide nectar and pollen.

Pesticides & safe practice

  • Avoid insecticides; never spray open blooms.
  • If treatment is unavoidable, apply at dusk, choose bee-friendly options, and follow the label exactly.
  • Source plants that are not pre-treated with systemic insecticides.

Making small spaces work

  • Container mixes: lavender + thyme + oregano for a balcony “bee bar”.
  • Succession in pots: swap out spent annuals to keep colour and nectar running.
  • Community verge/hedgerow projects extend forage beyond your garden.
