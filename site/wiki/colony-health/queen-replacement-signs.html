Queen replacement signs

How to recognise when a colony is preparing to replace its queen, and how to respond calmly and effectively.

Why queens are replaced

  • Natural ageing — pheromone output and egg-laying rate decline.
  • Damage or deformity — injury or missing limbs/antennae can trigger supersedure.
  • Colony imbalance — uneven brood pattern or reduced queen scent distribution.

Recognising queen cells

  • Supersedure cells: one or two, often mid-frame, well-fed, not numerous.
  • Swarm cells: many, usually along frame bottoms, in strong colonies pre-swarm.
  • Emergency cells: rough, quickly built, often at edges of open brood.

Location and number tell the story—supersedure is calm, swarm prep is energetic.

Timeline of replacement

  • Day 0–3: new eggs laid in queen cups.
  • Day 8: cells capped; old queen may remain until new one emerges.
  • Day 16: virgin queen emerges, dispatches rivals, then mates over next 1–2 weeks.
  • Day 25–30: new queen begins laying; brood pattern visible again within 10 days.

During inspection

  • Do not destroy single, centred supersedure cells—colonies usually know best.
  • If multiple large cells are charged and queen present, monitor weekly before intervening.
  • Mark old and new queens (different colours) to confirm successful transition later.

A temporary brood break is normal; patience prevents double-queen accidents.

After replacement

  • Watch for normal egg-laying within 2–3 weeks.
  • Assess temperament and brood pattern before deciding to requeen artificially.
  • Update colony records with new queen details and date of emergence.

When intervention may help

  • Emergency cells raised from very young larvae after accidental queen loss — consider uniting or introducing a mated queen.
  • Multiple poor supersedures in one season — underlying disease or genetic issues likely.
  • Colonies repeatedly failing to requeen — consider combining with a strong neighbour.
