Hygienic behaviour testing

Simple field tests to gauge how effectively a colony detects and removes unhealthy brood.

Why it matters

  • Hygienic colonies identify and remove diseased or mite-infested brood early.
  • Improves resistance to Varroa and brood diseases like AFB/EFB.
  • Supports breeding decisions by identifying resilient stock.

Common testing methods

  • Pin test: manually perforate capped brood cells and measure removal within 24 hours.
  • Liquid nitrogen test: kills a patch of capped brood; record removal after 24–48 hours.
  • Freeze-killed brood test: uses freezer-treated comb section reintroduced to the hive.

All methods measure removal rate: % of dead brood cleared in a fixed period.

Equipment checklist

  • Frame of sealed brood (flat, uniform area).
  • Marker to outline test patch (usually 100 cells).
  • Pin or needle; or liquid nitrogen cup (100 ml size).
  • Timer, notebook or phone app, and camera for documentation.

Running a pin test

  1. Select a flat area of sealed worker brood (approx. 10x10 cells).
  2. Pierce each capped cell vertically through the centre with a pin or fine needle.
  3. Mark the area edge with drawing pins or marker for reference.
  4. Return the frame to its original colony position.
  5. After 24 hours, count how many punctured cells have been uncapped and cleaned out.

Scoring: ≥95% removal = highly hygienic; 80–95% = moderate; <80% = low.

Running a liquid nitrogen test

  1. Cut the base from a drinks can to form a cylinder (~80 mm diameter).
  2. Press it into sealed brood comb to isolate a circular patch (~100 cells).
  3. Pour in 100 ml of liquid nitrogen to freeze-kill brood; allow to thaw.
  4. Return the frame to the colony; check after 24–48 hours.
  5. Count cells fully cleaned out of wax cappings and contents.

Use gloves and protective eyewear. Liquid nitrogen handling requires care.

Interpreting results

  • High removal rates correlate with better Varroa resistance and lower brood disease incidence.
  • Scores can vary with weather, nectar flow, and colony size.
  • Retest in a different season to confirm consistency.

Compare colonies under similar conditions — never between hives with different brood ages or flows.

Breeding and record-keeping

  • Mark queens from hygienic colonies; note scores, date, and test method.
  • Use results to select breeders or queens for splits.
  • Share data with local associations to strengthen regional breeding programmes.
