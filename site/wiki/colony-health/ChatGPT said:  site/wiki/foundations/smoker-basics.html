Smoker basics

Lighting, fuel choices, and using smoke calmly so inspections are quick and low-stress.

Why use smoke?

  • Masks alarm pheromone so guard bees don’t escalate.
  • Encourages bees to fill with nectar, making them less defensive.
  • Gives you a short “inspection window” with calmer frames.

Aim for light, cool smoke — think “two gentle puffs”, not fog.

Good fuels

  • Hessian/burlap (untreated), pine needles, wood pellets, rotten punk wood.
  • A spark starter: egg box/card, dry grass, or a paper twist at the bottom.
  • Avoid treated materials, glossy magazines, plastics, and resinous wood offcuts.

Lighting: quick method

  1. Loosen the grate and add a thumb-sized starter (card/egg box). Light it fully.
  2. Once it’s burning, add a small handful of main fuel; puff bellows to build a bright ember.
  3. Top up with more fuel, pack loosely for airflow, then close the lid. Aim for cool, white smoke.

If smoke turns hot/sharp, add more cool fuel and slow the puffs.

Using the smoker during inspections

  • At the entrance: two gentle puffs, wait 20–30 seconds.
  • Under the crown board: one puff then pause; avoid blasting brood directly.
  • As needed: a light puff across the top bars before lifting frames.

Less is more. Over-smoking can drive bees down and make finding the queen harder.

Safety & aftercare

  • Keep a metal stand and a dedicated, non-flammable rest area.
  • Carry water/sand; never leave a lit smoker in dry grass or a vehicle.
  • At the end, snuff with a cork/plug or invert and starve of oxygen; store ash cold.

Troubleshooting

  • Goes out quickly: fuel packed too tight; open airflow, use drier fuel.
  • Hot, acrid smoke: too little fuel over the ember; add cool fuel, slow puffs.
  • Sparks: fuel too dry/fine; add chunkier fuel, check grate and lid fit.
