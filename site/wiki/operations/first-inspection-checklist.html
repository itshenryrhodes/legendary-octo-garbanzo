First inspection checklist

A calm, step-by-step checklist for safe, useful hive inspections: what to prepare, what to look for, and when to stop.

Before you go

  • Weather OK (dry, >14 °C, light wind)? Midday is friendliest.
  • Kit packed: smoker, fuel, lighter; hive tool; clean veil/gloves.
  • Spare equipment: queen clip, markers, elastic bands, spare frames/box if needed.
  • Record sheet or app; camera for brood pattern photos.

At the hive (opening)

  • Stand behind or to one side of the entrance (out of the flight line).
  • Two gentle puffs at the entrance; wait 20–30 seconds.
  • Lift roof and crown board carefully; another light puff if needed.
  • Create a working gap: ease an outside frame sideways first.

The “3 Qs” — what to confirm

  • Queen-right? Look for eggs or very young larvae if the queen isn’t seen.
  • Quantity? Enough bees to cover brood and guard stores; note temperament.
  • Quality? Solid brood pattern with few holes; healthy pearly larvae, flat worker cappings.

If any “Q” is off, make a note and plan a follow-up; don’t overwork the colony today.

What to look for (frame by frame)

  • Eggs/larvae: single egg per cell, C-shaped larvae in jelly.
  • Brood pattern: mostly continuous; scattered drone brood on worker frames can signal queen issues.
  • Stores: nectar ring and pollen arcs; check weight by tilting the box.
  • Queen cells: charged cells or cups? Note position (bottom/face) and number.
  • Health signs: chalky mummies, ropey larval remains, or deformed wings → investigate.

Keep brood covered and shaded. Return frames gently—rolling bees risks injuring the queen.

When to stop

  • Bees becoming defensive; smoke no longer settles them.
  • Temperature drops, rain, or strong wind picks up.
  • You’ve answered your purpose (the 3 Qs) and taken notes.

Record the visit

  • Date, weather, colony strength, brood pattern notes, stores.
  • Any queen cells and action taken.
  • Next check date and specific follow-ups.
