Smoker basics

What a smoker does, how to light it reliably, which fuels to use, and how to apply cool, thin smoke so inspections stay calm and safe.

Why smoke works

Honey bees defend with alarm pheromones released near the sting site. Cool, thin smoke disrupts alarm signaling and encourages bees to retreat and gorge on nectar or honey, which makes stinging less likely. Historically this has been explained as smoke masking alarm pheromones (notably isopentyl acetate); modern reviews describe a combination of masking, sensory interference, and behavioral shift to ingestion and evacuation behavior. The effect is temporary—use sparingly and refresh only if needed.

Equipment & parts

Standard smoker

Stainless steel firebox, heat guard, bellows, lid with spout, and a grate at the bottom for airflow. Sizes vary; a medium smoker (approx. 4×10 in / 10×25 cm) suits most hobbyists.

Useful add-ons

  • Starter cube (plain, untreated wood fiber) for easy lighting.
  • Long lighter or matches.
  • Cork or metal cap to snuff the smoker when done.
  • Fireproof base (metal tray, bare soil) to rest a hot smoker.
  • Small spray bottle with water for spot-damping ash.

Good fuels (and what to avoid)

Choose clean, natural fuels that make cool, white/blue, non-acrid smoke:

  • Hessian/burlap (untreated), dried pine needles, wood shavings, punky wood, dry leaves, clean cardboard egg trays, or dried herb stems.
  • Avoid treated/painted/pressure-treated wood, glossy cardboard, plastics/synthetics, baler twine, cedar, or anything resinous or perfumed that can create irritant smoke.

Pack loosely at first for airflow, then top up after ignition to create a slow, steady smolder.

Lighting the smoker (step-by-step)

  1. Place the smoker on non-flammable ground. Open the lid; ensure the bottom grate is clear.
  2. Ignite a small wad of starter fuel. Pump the bellows gently until you have a stable flame.
  3. Add a handful of main fuel. Keep pumping until the flame works through the fuel.
  4. Pack more fuel in small increments. Alternate: add fuel → pump until flaming → add fuel.
  5. When smoke turns cool and dense, close the lid. Test on your wrist: it should feel warm, not hot.
  6. During inspections, top up small amounts to maintain a steady, cool smolder.

Tip: If smoke is hot or sharp, you’re burning, not smoldering. Open the lid, add more fuel, and reduce airflow with gentler puffs.

How to use smoke during inspections

  • At the entrance: 1–2 small puffs, then wait ~30–60 seconds. This sends a calm signal without flooding the hive.
  • Under the cover: After cracking the outer cover with your hive tool, give a light puff across the top bars and pause.
  • When moving frames: If bees crowd an area, waft a short puff above them and wait a few seconds.
  • Use as little as needed: Over-smoking can mask queen pheromones and make bees leave the brood area; aim for minimal, well-timed cues.

When not to use smoke

  • During heavy nectar dearth + robbing pressure: Use sparingly; consider switching to a light sugar-water mist to avoid triggering defensive drift. See Feeding basics.
  • On very windy, hot, or drought days: Fire risk may outweigh benefits. Postpone or use alternative calming methods.
  • With known respiratory sensitivities: Keep downwind, use less, or work without smoke if colonies are already calm.

Safety & fire risk

  • Clear a 3–5 m area of dry brush before lighting. Keep water or an extinguisher within reach.
  • Rest the smoker on bare soil or a metal stand—never on dry grass, wood, or a vehicle tailgate.
  • Do not dump embers. To snuff: insert a cork/plug, lay the smoker on its side to cut airflow, and allow to cool fully before cleaning.
  • Empty cold ash into a metal container with a lid. Store fuels dry, away from ignition sources.

Troubleshooting

Goes out constantly
Fuel packed too tight (no airflow) or too loose (no heat core). Re-light, then alternate fuel and bellows to build a hot core.
Smoke is hot/acrid
You have active flame or resinous fuel. Add clean fuel and reduce pumping until the flame drops to a smolder.
Bellows weak
Check for splits/leaks. Many bellows use replaceable leather or silicone; patch or replace as needed.
