Comb architecture basics

Comb is the colony’s living infrastructure: nursery, pantry, and walkway system. Reading its pattern helps you judge health, space, and next actions.

Why comb matters

Comb stores brood, nectar, honey, and pollen; guides traffic and thermoregulation; and records the colony’s history. Good comb supports efficient inspections and healthy growth.

Cell sizes & purposes

  • Worker cells (~5.2–5.4 mm) — majority of brood area; compact, even capping when healthy.
  • Drone cells (~6.2–6.6 mm) — larger, domed cappings; typically at frame edges or dedicated patches.
  • Storage cells — the same hexagons extended a little deeper for nectar/honey; pollen cells often rimmed with multicoloured granules (“bee bread”).

Foundation sheets bias size; natural (foundationless) comb shows more variation, especially early season.

Orientation & “bee space”

Bees keep a walkable gap (about 6–9 mm) between comb faces. Too little: they propolise; too much: they add burr/bridge comb. Straight comb and consistent spacing make inspections faster and gentler.

Typical frame layout

  • Center frames: worker brood with a pollen fringe and honey arc above (“rainbow frame”).
  • Outer frames: more nectar and capped honey, less brood.
  • Drone patches: at lower corners or edges, or on frames the colony has “chosen” for drones.

This pattern shifts with season and flow; heavy flows push nectar into brood areas until nurses reclaim cells after emergence.

Reading comb quality

  • Even brood pattern: few empty cells among capped worker brood suggests a well-mated queen.
  • Scattered brood (“shotgun”): consider queen age, nutrition, disease pressure; cross-check your varroa counts.
  • Excess drone comb: fine in spring; persistent excess can hint at queen issues or mis-spaced frames.
  • Cross/bridge comb: indicates spacing or level issues; correcting early prevents chronic damage during inspections.

Comb management basics

  1. Guide straight builds: keep frames tight together; use drawn comb as a ladder between new sheets.
  2. Rotate dark comb out: old black comb (heavy, brittle) can harbor residues and spores; cycle a few frames out each season.
  3. Add space at the right time: super before the main flow to prevent honey back-filling brood space (see Swarm signs & prevention).
  4. Fix cross comb early: on warm days, cut and press straight; secure with elastic bands if needed.

Foundation vs foundationless

Foundation: faster, straighter starts and predictable cell size; good for beginners. Foundationless: flexible, lighter equipment, and easy cut-comb; requires careful keeping frames plumb and tight.

Inspection cues

  • Fresh white comb: colony is in building mode; good time to expand.
  • Honey back-fill in brood nest: add space; check for swarm cues such as charged queen cups.
  • Uneven cappings or perforations: look for chilled brood, varroa damage, or hygienic removal (see Hygienic behaviour testing).

Handling to protect comb

  • Work when comb is warm enough to flex without tearing.
  • Lift frames vertically; don’t lever tight frames against soft comb faces.
  • Keep propolis off top bars so frames seat squarely and maintain bee space.
