Bee anatomy

External and internal structures that drive behaviour and colony work.

External features

  • Head: compound eyes for light and motion, antennae for smell and touch, proboscis for nectar.
  • Thorax: anchor point for wings and legs; flight muscles live here.
  • Abdomen: contains wax glands (workers), sting apparatus (workers/queen), and digestive organs.
  • Wing coupling: tiny hooks (hamuli) link fore and hind wings for efficient flight.
  • Pollen baskets: workers’ hind legs form corbiculae to carry pollen.

Internal systems

  • Digestive: honey stomach (crop) for nectar transport, midgut for digestion.
  • Circulatory: open system; haemolymph pumped by a dorsal vessel.
  • Respiratory: spiracles and tracheae deliver oxygen without lungs.
  • Nervous: brain and segmental ganglia coordinate complex behaviour.
  • Glands: mandibular, Nasonov, wax glands influence communication and work.

Worker, queen, drone — key differences

  • Worker: pollen baskets, wax glands, barbed sting; varied age-based roles.
  • Queen: longer abdomen for egg laying; smooth sting; pheromone production.
  • Drone: large eyes, no sting or pollen baskets; body built for mating flight.

See also: Bee castes & lifecycle.

Senses & communication

  • Smell: antennae detect pheromones for orientation, alarm, and queen presence.
  • Vision: ultraviolet patterns on flowers guide foraging.
  • Vibration: substrate-borne signals help coordinate work inside the hive.

Tip: scented markers (Nasonov pheromone) help returning foragers find the entrance in poor weather.

Practical beekeeping implications

  • Handling: avoid blocking spiracles; gentle smoke masks alarm pheromone.
  • Inspection: brood pattern and abdomen shape help assess queen status.
  • Foraging: full pollen baskets indicate strong forage; wax scale suggests comb building.
