Bee communication

How honeybees share information through dances, pheromones, and vibration — a social language evolved for survival.

Communication essentials

  • Bees coordinate complex behaviour with minimal sound and no sight inside the hive.
  • Information flows through chemical signals, touch, and movement.
  • Collective decisions (swarming, foraging, defence) depend on constant feedback loops.

Pheromones — chemical language

  • Queen mandibular pheromone: keeps colony cohesion, signals queen presence.
  • Brood pheromone: regulates worker roles and foraging drive.
  • Nasanov pheromone: guides orientation and home scent recognition.
  • Alarm pheromone: triggers defensive clustering and stinging response.

Each caste produces and responds to a unique mix — like chemical “sentences.”

Dance language

  • Waggle dance: direction and distance of forage sources relative to the sun.
  • Round dance: nearby nectar or water (<50 m from hive).
  • Bees interpret angle, tempo, and buzz duration to navigate precisely.

Discovered by Karl von Frisch — a milestone in animal behaviour science.

Vibrations & touch

  • Stop signal: a brief headbutt and buzz to interrupt dancers — regulates recruitment.
  • Tremble dance: adjusts forager-labour balance when nectar inflow exceeds processing rate.
  • Antennal contact conveys recognition, dominance, and colony mood.

Collective decision-making

  • Scout bees debate potential nest sites through waggle dances and inhibition signals.
  • Consensus builds when ~80% of scouts align — swarm then departs.
  • Demonstrates “distributed intelligence”: no single leader, only feedback.

Human parallels

  • Similar to neural networks — simple agents processing local signals to reach global consensus.
  • Inspired models for swarm robotics and AI collective behaviour.

Understanding bee communication helps decode resilience in complex systems.
