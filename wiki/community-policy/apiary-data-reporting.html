API Data Reporting Requirements

What beekeepers may need to report to agricultural authorities or programs—losses, diseases, Varroa levels, and more—and how to build a simple, compliant reporting workflow.

← Back to Community & Policy hub

What this page covers (and what it doesn’t)

This page explains the typical types of information a beekeeper may be asked to submit to public agencies or industry programs, plus practical ways to prepare, collect, and transmit those data. It is jurisdiction-agnostic and does not offer legal advice; always check your local or national authority for specific rules and deadlines. Use this as a blueprint to build a lean, repeatable reporting workflow that also improves day-to-day management.

Why reporting exists

  • Public animal health surveillance: Detect outbreaks of notifiable diseases (e.g., American foulbrood) and track parasite trends such as Varroa.
  • Risk management: Inform pesticide application windows, pollination contracts, and movement controls.
  • Evidence for policy: Loss and health data shape guidance, extension funding, and research priorities.

Done well, reporting returns value to beekeepers: regional risk alerts, better extension materials, and more realistic timelines for interventions.

Common reporting programs and triggers

  • Annual or seasonal loss surveys: Self-reported winter and in-season mortality, colony counts, and management factors.
  • Notifiable disease reporting: Mandatory notification if you suspect specific diseases or pests identified by your authority.
  • Movement and registration updates: Apiary registration changes, hive movements across borders, or pollination placement declarations.
  • Pesticide incident reporting: Suspected pesticide kills or sublethal effects near treated crops (see Reporting Pesticide Incidents).
  • Program-specific monitoring: Voluntary initiatives requesting standardized Varroa counts, pathogen screening, or nutrition metrics to produce regional dashboards.

The core dataset most programs ask for

Typical fields are predictable. If you standardize how you collect these, most forms become “fill and ship.”

  • Operation profile: Beekeeper ID/registration number, contact details, scale (number of colonies).
  • Apiary locations: Site IDs and coarse location (e.g., county/region or lat/long with optional obfuscation for privacy).
  • Colony counts by period: Start-of-season, end-of-season, splits made, merges, and purchases.
  • Losses: Winter and in-season losses, suspected causes (starvation, queen failure, Varroa-associated, pesticides, unknown).
  • Varroa monitoring: Methods (alcohol wash, sugar roll, sticky board), sample size, counts (% infestation) by month (see Varroa monitoring schedule).
  • Disease diagnostics: Positive results or strong suspicions for notifiable diseases (see Colony Diagnostic Tests and American Foulbrood Disease).
  • Treatments: Active ingredients, timing, reason (threshold-based, calendar, emergency), and application method (link to Varroa Treatment).
  • Nutrition and forage: Feeding events (syrup, fondant, pollen patties), dearth notes, and significant forage changes (see Planting for pollinators).
  • Pollination services: Crop types, placement windows, and observed risks.

Tip: Keep the dataset minimal but longitudinal: fewer fields, recorded consistently, are more powerful than sporadic detail.

Build a reporting workflow that runs itself

  1. Define your master schema: Mirror the fields above in a single spreadsheet or app profile. Use consistent names and units (e.g., Varroa as “mites/100 bees”). See Data Management for Beekeepers.
  2. Choose your capture tools: Paper cards, a phone app, or both. If you are new, start with a one-page card clipped to each yard and transcribe weekly. See Record-Keeping for Hives.
  3. Set inspection cadence: Weekly in buildup, biweekly in shoulder seasons. Align Varroa counts with your monitoring schedule.
  4. Monthly consolidation: Roll apiary cards into the master sheet at month-end; run simple checks (missing values, outliers).
  5. Quarterly snapshots: Export the fields that external programs typically ask for (counts, losses, Varroa, treatments).
  6. Submission binder: Keep PDF exports, submission confirmations, and any case numbers for disease or incident reports.

If you already use digital systems, map your fields once and stick to them. Small operations can still benefit from a simple, printable template (see example below).

Example minimal data template

Use this as your “always-there” sheet on a clipboard or as columns in your app/spreadsheet.

  • Apiary ID · County/Region
  • Date · Weather (brief) · Bloom notes
  • Colonies inspected · Colonies alive
  • Varroa check? (Y/N) · Method · Result (mites/100 bees)
  • Queen status (seen, eggs, pattern)
  • Treatments this period (AI, dose, reason)
  • Losses this period (count) · Suspected cause
  • Feeding (type/amount) · Dearth notes
  • Incidents (pesticide exposure, vandalism, bears)

Data quality & defensibility

  • Standardize methods: Use the same Varroa method each time for trend comparability; note deviations.
  • Time-stamp and sign: For paper cards, initial each line. For apps, ensure automatic timestamps are visible.
  • Chain of evidence: Keep photos of diagnostic frames, test strips, and sample labels for notifiable disease cases.
  • Version control: Don’t overwrite raw entries; append corrections with a short reason (“transcription fix”).

Privacy, location precision, and sharing

Reporting programs balance surveillance with beekeeper privacy. A practical rule is to store precise coordinates in your private records, then supply the minimum required public precision (e.g., county or rounded lat/long) unless law requires exact points. If contributing to open dashboards (see Open Data in Beekeeping), verify how location is generalized and who can view it.

  • Obfuscation: Round coordinates to ~1–5 km for public maps unless exact is required for control zones.
  • Data separation: Keep your contact list and customer details in a separate file from health datasets.
  • Access logs: If you use online forms, confirm who can read submissions and how long they’re retained.

Submission formats and tips

Most programs accept web forms, email attachments (CSV/PDF), or occasional paper mail-ins. A few accept API or app-to-app uploads via partner platforms. To speed your work:

  • Keep a “report-ready” export view: Pre-filter your sheet to the fields programs request.
  • Use consistent file names: YYYY-MM_ApiarySummary_YourName.csv and …_LossSurvey.pdf.
  • Readable PDF: If forms are scanned, use high contrast and a dark pen; attach photos as separate files when allowed.
  • Confirm receipt: Save auto-receipts or email replies to your submission binder.

Special cases and decision points

Suspected notifiable disease
Isolate suspect hives, limit robbing, and follow your authority’s notification pathway immediately. Keep photos and sample IDs. Review Colony Diagnostic Tests.
Pollination contracts
Growers may request pre-placement Varroa counts, queen status, and loss history. Provide only the agreed fields; keep full records privately.
Pesticide incidents
Document rapidly: wide and close photos, dead bee samples, weather, spray times, applicator contact if known. File an incident report (see Reporting Pesticide Incidents) and notify neighbors (see Pesticide Application Notification).

Lightweight automation (optional)

Without getting “too tech,” you can gain reliability with small automations:

  • Calendar nudges: Reminders for monthly consolidations and seasonal survey windows.
  • Drop-in forms: A single mobile form that logs to your master sheet with standardized dropdowns.
  • Device data: If you use hive scales or sensors (see Hive Weight Monitoring and Hive Temperature and Humidity Sensors), export monthly summaries and attach them to your binder.

Pre-submission checklist

  • All apiaries listed with consistent IDs and regions.
  • Colony counts balanced (start + splits − losses ± sales = end).
  • At least one Varroa data point per month in buildup months (method noted).
  • Treatments logged with active ingredient and reason.
  • Any disease positives documented with photo or lab ref.
  • Files exported in the requested format; submission receipt saved.

Frequently asked questions

What if I don’t know a cause of loss?
Use “unknown” rather than guessing. Add a short note—e.g., “light stores; Varroa not checked since Aug.”
Do small backyard beekeepers have to report?
Sometimes only for notifiable diseases or if registered; some surveys are voluntary. Check your local rules; voluntary data are still valuable for regional pictures.
Will sharing locations increase theft risk?
Share the minimum precision required publicly. Keep exact coordinates private unless control measures require them.
How do I stay consistent across seasons?
Lock your schema, keep methods the same, and use a monthly consolidation habit. Consistency beats complexity.
