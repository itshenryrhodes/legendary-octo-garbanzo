Colony Diagnostic Tests

Practical field and lab-style tests to diagnose common honey bee problems—mites, brood diseases, nosema, and viruses—and how to sample, interpret, and act safely.

← Back to Colony Health

Why diagnostics matter

Good decisions come from good measurements. Colonies can fail for very different reasons that look similar from outside the box. A repeatable diagnostic workflow—paired with clear record-keeping—lets you identify the primary stressor, choose the least disruptive response, and verify that your action worked. This page focuses on the how of testing: what to sample, the tools you need, and how to interpret results alongside signs seen on frames.

Use this article with Colony Health Assessment for visual signs, with Varroa Monitoring Schedule for timing and thresholds, and with disease pages such as American Foulbrood, European Foulbrood, Nosema, and Deformed Wing Virus.

Designing a sampling plan

  • Objective first: Are you screening routine health (spring/fall), investigating specific symptoms, or verifying treatment efficacy? Your goal determines which tests to run.
  • Representative bees: For parasite/pathogen screening, sample adult nurse bees from brood frames (not foragers at the entrance). Nurses carry higher pathogen and mite loads.
  • Replicates: In yards with many hives, test a subset but include any colony that looks atypical (spotty brood, unusual odor, crawling bees, deformed wings).
  • Label and log: Assign a sample ID, date, colony ID, yard, weather, and notes. Use a consistent sheet or app (apps for beekeepers, apiary management software).
  • Biosecurity: Use clean gloves and tools between hives, flame your hive tool if investigating foulbrood, and double-bag suspect samples.

Varroa mite testing

Because Varroa destructor drives much of modern colony loss, mite testing is the most valuable routine diagnostic. Choose one primary method and repeat it consistently each time so results are comparable.

Alcohol wash (gold standard)

  • What it measures: Estimated mite load per 100 bees by dislodging mites.
  • Materials: Jar with mesh lid, 70% isopropyl alcohol (or ethanol), measuring scoop, white tray.
  • Sample: ~1/2 cup (≈300) nurse bees shaken from an open-brood frame into a container; avoid the queen.
  • Procedure: Cover bees with alcohol, swirl 60 seconds, drain through mesh into tray, refill and repeat; count mites in tray. Count bees or compute by volume.
  • Result: Report as mites per 100 bees. Compare to your seasonal thresholds.

Sugar roll (non-lethal)

  • What it measures: Dislodged mites using powdered sugar.
  • Materials: Jar with mesh lid, confectioners’ sugar, white tray, water spray bottle.
  • Procedure: Add ~300 bees to jar, 2 tbsp sugar, roll for 60 seconds, rest 1 minute, shake mites onto tray; mist to dissolve sugar for easier counting.
  • Caveats: Accuracy depends on humidity and technique; still useful when alcohol wash is impractical.

Sticky board (drop counts)

  • What it measures: Natural or induced mite drop per day on a sticky board under a screened bottom.
  • Use case: Trending before/after treatments. Less reliable for absolute infestation compared to wash/roll.

Drone brood uncapping

  • What it shows: Infestation inside capped drone cells. Helps time drone brood removal tactics; not a substitute for adult-bee sampling.

Diagnosing brood diseases

Abnormal brood patterns can arise from nutrition, temperature, queen age, and pathogens. Start with inspection cues, then confirm with rapid tests or lab submission as needed.

American foulbrood (AFB)

  • Field signs: Sunken perforated cappings, coffee-brown larval remains, characteristic odor in advanced cases, “rope” test from cell contents.
  • Rapid test kits: Commercial lateral-flow immunoassays can detect AFB antigens from a swab of suspicious cells.
  • Confirmatory: Lab culture or PCR where available. Handle suspect frames carefully; avoid spreading spores between hives.
  • Next steps: Follow your jurisdiction’s guidance; see AFB and Apiary Inspections.

European foulbrood (EFB)

  • Field signs: Uncapped or poorly capped larvae that appear twisted or melted, color from white to yellowish; no ropey consistency.
  • Rapid test kits: Lateral-flow kits exist for EFB as well.
  • Context: Often associated with nutritional stress; improving forage and requeening can help. See EFB.

Chalkbrood

  • Field signs: Hard, chalky mummies (white to black) in cells or on the bottom board.
  • Diagnosis: Usually clinical based on mummies; lab microscopy can confirm.
  • Next steps: Reduce stress, improve ventilation, replace old combs; see Chalkbrood Disease.

Nosema testing (microscopy)

Nosema apis and Nosema ceranae infect adult bees’ midgut. A quick spore count guides whether management changes are warranted.

  • Sample: 25–60 adult bees (preferably older workers from inside the hive). Freeze if not processing immediately.
  • Preparation: Homogenize abdomens in a measured volume of water (e.g., 1 mL per bee) using a mortar or zip-bag and roller.
  • Microscopy: Place a drop on a slide; count spores using 400×. Report as spores per bee (convert from hemocytometer counts).
  • Interpretation: Look for trends across time and season rather than a single cutoff. Correlate with dysentery, reduced longevity, and poor build-up; see Nosema Disease.

Virus screening

Common viruses (DWV, IAPV, CBPV, BQCV) are ubiquitous at low levels; high titers usually track with Varroa pressure.

  • When to test: If colonies show neurologic signs (trembling, hairless shiny bees), deformed wings not explained by mite counts, or unexplained autumn dwindle.
  • How: Submit preserved adult samples to a bee lab for PCR/RT-qPCR quantification. Track results with concurrent mite data to understand cause, not just correlation.
  • Acting on results: Focus on reducing Varroa and improving nutrition; antiviral treatments are not standard practice. See DWV.

Suspected pesticide exposure

Field clues include piles of fresh dead bees at the entrance, disoriented foragers, or brood unaffected initially. If you suspect exposure:

  1. Collect 200–300 fresh dead bees in solvent-rinsed containers (no wax or propolis) and keep cool.
  2. Note crop bloom, recent sprays, wind, and water sources. Photograph labels if safe and appropriate.
  3. Contact your extension or testing lab for chain-of-custody and shipping instructions.
  4. Consult Pesticide Application Notification and Risk Management Plans.

Interpreting results and acting

Numbers are only useful when tied to thresholds and a playbook. Examples:

  • Mites per 100 bees: Compare to seasonal thresholds in your monitoring schedule. If over threshold, select a treatment compatible with weather, brood status, and honey supers; see treatment options.
  • AFB/EFB positive: Follow regulatory guidance. For AFB, equipment handling is critical; for EFB, improve nutrition and consider a management/labeled medication plan where permitted.
  • High Nosema trend: Emphasize forage quality, reduce stress, and rotate older comb; medications are jurisdiction-specific and not always indicated.
  • Virus titers high with mites low: Re-verify mite counts (repeat alcohol wash). If still low, check for nutrition gaps and queen performance.

A simple yard workflow

  1. Visual triage: On opening, score cluster size, brood pattern, food frames, moisture, and odors. Note wing deformities or trembling bees.
  2. Mite test: Perform an alcohol wash or sugar roll on a brood-frame sample.
  3. Brood spot checks: If brood is patchy or discolored, collect targeted photos and, if warranted, use a rapid foulbrood test.
  4. Nosema sample (optional): If spring build-up is poor or dysentery present, collect bees for microscopy.
  5. Record decisions: Log test values and the action (treat, feed, requeen, recheck). Schedule a follow-up test 7–14 days after interventions where appropriate.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Sampling the wrong bees: Entrance foragers understate parasite loads; always target nurses on open brood.
  • Inconsistent methods: Switching between sugar roll and alcohol wash makes trend lines noisy. Pick one and stick to it.
  • Too few bees: Small samples inflate error. Aim for ~300 adults for mite testing.
  • Counting bias: Use a white tray and good light; recount if uncertain.
  • No baseline: Without pre-treatment data, you can’t verify efficacy. Test before and after.
  • Cross-contamination: Reuse of gloves/tools on suspect foulbrood frames spreads spores; flame or replace tools.

Recommended diagnostic kit

  • Alcohol wash jar with mesh lid (or sugar roll jar) and measuring scoop.
  • Powdered sugar and clean isopropyl alcohol (70%).
  • White catch tray or pan; squeeze bottle for water (sugar roll).
  • Rapid test kits for AFB/EFB (kept sealed, check expiry).
  • Sample vials, labels, and permanent marker.
  • Thick nitrile gloves and lighter/torch for tool sanitation when needed.
  • Optional: Portable microscope/hemocytometer for Nosema counts.
